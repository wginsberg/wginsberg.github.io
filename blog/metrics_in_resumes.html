Putting metrics on resumes is baffling

I have seen the same resume advice repeated many times which encourages "quantifying" one's impact with numbers. Something like:

"Improved data processing speed by 20%, enhancing application performance and user experience."

This advice bothers me1 for a bunch of reasons that I want to list here:

  1. They are not verifiable

    If you want to put in the effort you can likely verify a candidates entire employment history and diplomas. What about metrics? Does Steve's manager from his internship two years ago remember when he improved data processing speed by 20%? Was it really just 10%? Are we seriously going to find a record of that when we check his references?

  2. They are not comparable

    Aren't numbers great? They help us compare things in the real world. What kind of comparison can we make between Alice, who improved page load times by 20%, and Bob, who improved test coverage by 200%? A non-sensical comparison.

  3. They don't account for tradeoffs

    I decreased API response times from 2.87 seconds to 1.29 seconds (but I also doubled the rates of HTTP 500 errors). Next!

  4. They don't account for code quality

    I love this article from Martin Fowler about code quality. My takeaway from it is that there is a false tradeoff between getting the work done and maintaining high code quality. So what if you improved data processing speed by 20%? Did you make the code more difficult to read? To maintain? Of course we have no idea what happened next. Maybe it took your succesor a huge amount of unnecessary effort to further reduce data processing speed because of your bad code quality.

  5. They don't account for teamwork

    How can we divide the credit among members of a team when there is a big win - like when the project is delivered on time with a user satification rate of 82%? Again, maybe it doesn't even make sense to try to use mathematical concepts to analyze teamwork. Or maybe you contributed negative value to that team ðŸ«¢ 2

  6. It is easy to lie with statistics (even by accident)

    Didn't someone write a book about this?. Unless you are hiring a statistican, I don't think the mere idea that the candidate accidentally lied with a statistic is unforgivable. But then what is the point of these numbers? An example:

    "rewrote a monolithic application with a microservice architecture resulting in 50% faster query response time".
    Did the change of architecture actually have anything to do with the decreased response time? Why are we ignoring the distribution of response times? They could be trying to decieve us, or they could just be a little sloppy with talking in numbers.

Conclusion: Was this rant actually an advertisement?

If we are going to pretend to care about quantifiable achievements, maybe we should favor some kind of controlled experiment where candidates can be compared in an objective way? I didn't set out trying to write an advertisement for Karat but I truly think it is the logical answer to the problems with resumes. It actually delivers on the promise of hiring in tech: that it is a true meritocracy. It's not about who you know, or which school you went to. Anyone can prove their worth based on their demonstrated abilities, not their ability to talk about their abilities.
